# My draft email (DO NOT COPY EXACTLY)

Members of the committee,

I write to oppose HB 1023 which would seek to authorize accessory commercial buildings by right in residential neighborhoods.

While some of the ideas expressed in the bill are reasonable, and deserve formal protection, and are - indeed - already practices in much of NH (e.g. tax preparers and others working out of their homes), much of the bill is bad.

First, it's unclear what problem, if any, it solves.  While there is
widespread discussion in the state about how housing is expensive, I
have never once heard "in the wild" the statement "gee, I wish someone
would open a nail salon next door ... ideally one with no parking, so
that customers would park on the street".

A full list of my objections to this bill:

- It removes local control.  Mixed commerce might be the right choice for some neighborhoods; that doesn't mean it is for every neighborhood.  
- In libertarian / economic terms, it ignores Hayekian local knowledge.  Having lived in Weare for over a dozen years I know that certain roads do not meet state standards and are too narrow, and are grandfathered in...and that these roads become almost impassible if someone parks on one side.  Working in the context of my local town, I can talk to the planning board and zoning board and use this local knowledge.  Representatives in Concord have no data about small but important details in Weare and should not try to impose a Great Leap Forward on us.
- This bill does not compensate for externalities.  If customers to the new nail salon next door block my driveway, or on street parking makes travel more cumbersome, too bad.  Profits are internalized ; costs are externalized / socialized.
- Anyone except a 16 year old newly minted libertarian who read Ayn Rand for the first time understands that different land uses have different impacts.  Commercial use comes with an increased possibility for increased littering, theft, and trespassing.
- The benefits are speculative.
- The bill provides no mechanism to measure adverse impacts.
- ...and there are no mechanism for abutters to collect damages if there are adverse impacts.  
- The bill allows activities until late into the night (10pm) and very early in the morning (6am).
- The bill explicitly intends for on-street parking.
- There is no effective upper limit on the size of retail structures.  The language "greater of ... 25% of principle structure" allows for huge stores in, e.g. newly constructed steel industrial buildings, as long as the remainder is dedicated to, say, general storage (put in an 8,000 square foot steel building on a residential lot, use 6,000 square feet as garage and warehouse space, have a 2,000 square foot restaurant in the front)
- There is no mechanism for repairing damage if the predictions of the bill sponsors turn out to be wrong and the predictions of critics are right.  "Oops, we were wrong" might technically be an apology, but once a use has been created, it will be grandfathered.  Destroying, say, $200,000 of market value from my home on a rustic rural road because a new commercial venture goes into a building next door, 12 feet off my property lines isn't fixed by "sorry".

I could support various alternative versions of this bill.  Some fixes:

- allow only quiet, minimal traffic, white-collar occupations like tax preparation, etc. 
- require that off-street parking be required
- allow towns to restrict hours as tightly as 9am and 5pm
- give towns more control by allowing them to restrict the the "mixed commerce" zones to as little as 10% of residential areas.

The last one is incredibly important.  Top-down, big-bang changes are terrible, arrogant, and avoid all feedback loops.

If an idea is good, a small demonstration to let people see how good it is, to get the beneficiaries themselves to argue for growth, is a no-brainer.

100%, 60 days notice, big bang changes are terrible.  Good ideas don't need them.

Travis Corcoran
Hillsborough 28


# Grok version 1

Subject: Strong Opposition to HB 1023 – Establishing Accessory Commercial Units by Right

Dear Representative [Last Name],

I am writing as a concerned citizen to urge you to vote **NO** on House Bill 1023, which would mandate the establishment of accessory commercial units (ACUs) "by right" in every zoning district across New Hampshire. While I appreciate efforts to support small businesses and local economies, this bill represents an overreach of state authority that removes meaningful local control and exposes residential neighborhoods to significant unintended consequences without adequate safeguards.

HB 1023 overrides local zoning decisions by requiring municipalities to permit commercial operations—such as coffee shops, cafés, bakeries, salons, daycare centers, and small retail stores—as accessory uses on any lot with a principal residential structure. This top-down approach disregards the unique character, needs, and local knowledge of individual communities, contrary to the principle that those closest to an issue are best positioned to address it.

Key concerns with the bill include:

- **Erosion of Local Control** — By prohibiting municipalities from banning ACUs (except in limited ways), the bill strips towns of their ability to tailor zoning to protect neighborhood quality of life. Communities should retain authority over what commercial activities are appropriate in residential areas.

- **Potential for Significant Negative Impacts** — These units could generate increased traffic, noise until 10:00 p.m., and early morning activity starting at 6:00 a.m. The bill requires no additional off-street parking, which would likely lead to greater reliance on on-street parking and congestion in residential streets. Neighbors could face higher risks of littering, trespassing, theft, or other nuisances from customer foot traffic, with no built-in mechanisms to measure or mitigate these adverse effects.

- **Lack of Effective Size Limits** — The bill caps ACU floor area at the greater of 1,000 square feet or 25% of the principal structure's gross floor area. This creates a loophole: on a lot with a large principal building (e.g., an 8,000 square foot structure used partly for storage or garage space), an ACU could reach 2,000 square feet—enough for a substantial commercial operation disproportionate to a residential setting.

- **No Recourse for Affected Residents** — The bill provides no mechanism for abutters to seek compensation for externalities or damages if problems arise. It also lacks requirements for impact studies or evidence demonstrating statewide benefits, leaving communities to bear unproven risks.

New Hampshire's strength lies in its local governance and community-driven decisions. HB 1023 undermines this by imposing a uniform mandate without sufficient protections or evidence of net positive outcomes.

I respectfully ask that you oppose HB 1023 and vote **NO** when it comes before you. Protecting residential neighborhoods and preserving local authority are essential to maintaining the quality of life that makes New Hampshire special.

Thank you for your time and consideration. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this further.

# Grok Version 2

Subject: Please Vote NO on HB 1023 – Mandating Accessory Commercial Units Statewide Removes Local Control and Harms Neighborhoods

Dear Representative [Last Name],

I am a concerned citizen writing to urge you to vote NO on HB 1023 (2026 session), which would mandate "accessory commercial units" (ACUs) by right in every zoning district across New Hampshire, including residential neighborhoods. While the intent may be to support small businesses, this top-down legislation strips towns of meaningful local control, ignores the unique knowledge of local communities (the Hayekian principle that those closest to a problem best understand it), and exposes residents to uncompensated negative externalities without any evidence that benefits outweigh the harms.

HB 1023 would force municipalities to allow a wide range of commercial operations—coffee shops, cafés, bakeries, hair/nail salons, daycare centers, yoga studios, professional offices, small retail stores, and dozens of other uses—on any lot with a principal residential structure, either inside the home or in a detached building. Key problems include:

Complete Erosion of Local Control: The bill explicitly prohibits towns from banning or reasonably restricting ACUs, allowing only minimal registration/inspection rules that cannot be "more restrictive" than the state mandate. This overrides decades of community-driven zoning decisions.
No Effective Size Cap: Floor area is limited to the greater of 1,000 sq ft or 25% of the principal structure. This creates an enormous loophole: someone could construct an 8,000 sq ft steel building on a residential lot, dedicate 6,001 sq ft to garage/warehouse/storage (as the "principal" residential use), and operate a 2,000 sq ft restaurant, salon, or store in the remaining space—completely out of scale with any neighborhood.
Increased Traffic, Noise, and Nuisance Without Mitigation: Businesses could operate from 6:00 a.m. to 10:00 p.m. daily. No additional off-street parking is required, deliberately shifting parking burden onto residential streets. There is no requirement for traffic studies, no mechanism to measure or limit littering, trespassing, noise, or theft, and no way for affected neighbors to seek compensation or damages if problems arise.
No Evidence of Statewide Benefits, No Safeguards: The bill provides no data showing need or net positive impact, yet imposes one-size-fits-all rules on every community from Nashua suburbs to rural North Country towns. Predictions of harm come with no recourse or remediation process if they prove correct.
New Hampshire's tradition of local governance is one of our state's greatest strengths. Forcing commercial activity into residential areas against the wishes of the people who live there is not conservative, not pro-business in a sustainable way, and not respectful of property rights—it is heavy-handed state overreach.

Please stand with New Hampshire homeowners and vote NO on HB 1023. Protect local control and neighborhood quality of life.

Thank you for your consideration. I am happy to discuss this further.
